# Email Marketing for Therapists: Tools, Tips and HIPAA Considerations

You’ve built a small caseload, your website is live, and now you’re wondering how to keep in touch with past clients or attract new ones without violating privacy rules. Email feels like the obvious channel—until you remember that HIPAA (or GDPR, PIPEDA, UK-GDPR) applies the moment a name and any health context sit together in a list. I’ve spent the last two years testing setups for my own solo practice and helping colleagues stay compliant across the US, UK, Canada and Australia. This post walks you through two mainstream platforms—Mailchimp and ConvertKit—showing exactly what you can and can’t do with each, how to set them up, and what to write so you stay on the right side of the regulators.

> Quick verdict: If you need a signed HIPAA Business Associate Agreement, Mailchimp is your only option between the two. ConvertKit refuses BAAs, so use it only if every subscriber is outside the US or you are comfortable relying on consent-plus-GDPR workflows.

## Why Email Marketing Still Wins for Solo Therapy Practices

Referrals from doctors and past clients are great, but they’re unpredictable. Social media reach keeps shrinking unless you pay to boost posts. Email, by contrast, gives you a direct line to people who already raised their hand—either they booked a consult, downloaded a resource, or attended a workshop. When I shifted from sporadic Instagram posts to a simple monthly newsletter segmented by service interest (anxiety, EMDR, couples), my re-booking rate jumped 18 % within six months. The average open rate for mental-health lists hovers around 35 %, almost double the cross-industry average, because subscribers *want* coping tools delivered to their inbox. Done correctly, email keeps you top-of-mind without expensive ads or daily posting. The sticking point is compliance: once an address is paired with any clinical detail—"Sarah, anxiety client"—you’ve created PHI (protected health information) and encryption plus a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) become mandatory in the US. Similar rules apply under GDPR, UK-GDPR, PIPEDA and Australia’s Privacy Act. The good news is that the tech setup is a one-day project if you follow the checklist below.

## Who Should Choose Which

Pick **Mailchimp** if you:  
- See clients in the US and want a BAA to cover encrypted storage and transmission  
- Need advanced A/B testing or multivariate campaigns to fill group workshops  
- Already use IntakeQ, SimplePractice or other vendors that offer native Mailchimp zaps  

Pick **ConvertKit** if you:  
- Work only with UK, AU or CA clients and can rely on double-opt-in consent plus GDPR/Privacy-Shield clauses  
- Want the simplest automation editor possible and hate feature bloat  
- Plan to monetise courses or paid newsletters alongside therapy services  

If you straddle both regions, the safest route is Mailchimp with strict segmentation: US subscribers under the BAA, non-US subscribers under GDPR tags. Yes, you’ll pay slightly more, but one audit letter will cost far more than the monthly fee difference.

## Step-by-Step Setup Checklist (Works on Either Platform)

1. Create a new audience/list dedicated to marketing; never mix it with clinical records.  
2. Turn on double-opt-in; add a checkbox that reads "I consent to receive mental-health tips by email and understand I can unsubscribe at any time."  
3. Insert GDPR or CASL language in the footer; include your physical address for CAN-SPAM/CASL compliance.  
4. Build encrypted opt-in forms using Jotform HIPAA or Typeform Enterprise; connect via Zapier or the platform’s native integration.  
5. Tag by service interest (anxiety, EMDR, couples). Keep tags generic; never use "depressed client" or other PHI.  
6. Draft a five-email nurture sequence: welcome, bust two common myths, share three blog links, invite to book, request testimonial.  
7. Schedule sends between Tuesday and Thursday, 08:00–10:00 local time; subject lines ≤45 characters.  
8. Every quarter, export and delete bounced or unsubscribed contacts; store the CSV in an encrypted folder separate from your EHR.  
9. Run a re-consent campaign annually for EU/UK subscribers.  
10. Document everything—consent logs, BAA, data-processing agreements—in case of an audit.

- "EMDR explained in plain English"

Keep each email under 250 words, finish with a single call-to-action, and link to a secure blog post or booking page. Never embed client stories unless you have written consent and strip all identifiers.

## Common Compliance Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

**Pitfall 1:** Uploading your entire client spreadsheet "just to get started."  
**Fix:** Import only people who expressly opted in; otherwise you breach CASL, CAN-SPAM and GDPR.

**Pitfall 2:** Using a personal Gmail alias when someone hits reply with clinical info.  
**Fix:** Disable replies or route them to a HIPAA/GDPR-compliant help desk like Hushmail for Healthcare.

**Pitfall 3:** Forgetting to sign the Mailchimp BAA because you "only send newsletters."  
**Fix:** The moment you hold name + any health context, you need the BAA; complete it under Account > Compliance.

**Pitfall 4:** Assuming double-opt-in is enough for US clients.  
**Fix:** You still need the BAA; consent alone doesn’t satisfy the Security Rule.

**Pitfall 5:** Storing exported CSVs on an unencrypted laptop.  
**Fix:** Use an encrypted drive or cloud bucket with AES-256; treat the file like any other PHI.

## FAQ

### Do I need a BAA with Mailchimp if I only send a newsletter?

Yes, if you are a covered entity in the US and the list contains name + any health-related data—even "interested in anxiety support" counts as PHI. Mailchimp will sign a BAA only on paid plans; without it you breach the HIPAA Security Rule.

### Can I use ConvertKit if my clients are in the US?

You can, but you must ensure no PHI ever enters the platform because ConvertKit will not sign a BAA. That usually means relying on explicit consent and storing only name + generic tags like "workshop attendee." Most clinicians find the risk too high if they also treat US clients; non-US clients are lower risk under GDPR.

### What counts as PHI in an email list?

Any data that identifies the individual and relates to their physical or mental health. Name + email alone is not PHI, but name + email + "struggles with panic attacks" is. Keep your tags broad and avoid loading clinical notes.

### How do I collect consent under CASL/UK-GDPR?

Use a clear checkbox at the point of signup, keep a timestamped record, and send a confirmation email (double-opt-in). Include your organisation name, purpose of the messages, and a withdrawal method in every footer. Refresh consent every 12–24 months for inactive subscribers.

### What happens if someone replies with clinical info?

Route replies to a secure, encrypted mailbox covered by a BAA or GDPR data-processing agreement. Do not forward the message to an unsecured device. Document the incident and, if the data is sensitive, notify the individual within 72 h per breach-notification rules.
